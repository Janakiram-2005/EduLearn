Physics: Friction
Information

Friction: The Force That Opposes Motion

Friction is a force that resists the relative motion between surfaces in contact. It's the "grip" that allows us to walk, for cars to drive, and for brakes to work. It is caused by microscopic imperfections on surfaces that interlock with each other.

Types of Friction

  • Static Friction: The force that prevents an object from starting to move. It's the strongest type of friction.
  • Kinetic (Sliding) Friction: The force that acts on an object once it is already moving. It is weaker than static friction.
  • Rolling Friction: The resistance that occurs when a round object (like a ball or wheel) rolls on a surface. It is much weaker than sliding friction.
  • Fluid Friction (Drag): The friction that occurs in liquids or gases (like air resistance). It opposes the motion of objects moving through them.

Factors Affecting Friction

  • Nature of Surfaces: Rough surfaces (like sandpaper) have much higher friction than smooth surfaces (like ice).
  • Normal Force: The harder the surfaces are pressed together (i.e., the heavier the object), the greater the friction.

Advantages & Disadvantages

  • Advantages (Good Friction): Helps us walk without slipping, allows car brakes to work, lets us write with a pencil.
  • Disadvantages (Bad Friction): Causes wear and tear on machine parts, produces unwanted heat, makes it harder to move heavy objects.
